Abstract

Assessment of medical images and diagnostic decision making of lumbar associated diseases by clinicians is invariably subjective, time consuming and challenging task. Presently, clinicians make use of either manual or semi-automated computer-aided tools to make relevant measurements for adding vote of confidence to their grading and evaluation. Lacking reliability and offering substantive dissimilarity once performed by different clinicians, these methods complicate the evaluation process.

In an effort to support the decision making process of clinicians, in this paper we present a lumbar assessment framework with autonomous extraction of spinal measurements. Furthermore, an effort is made to address the challenges faced by clinicians while assessing disorders including spondylolisthesis and assessment of lumbar lordosis (LL) by proposing novel disease classification methodologies.

Key Results:
  • Spondylolisthesis Classification: 89% accuracy using angular deviation metric
  • Lumbar Lordosis Assessment: 93% accuracy through area within enclosed lumbar curve region
  • Segmentation Performance: DSC of 0.97 and IoU of 0.86 using ResNet-UNet
  • Measurement Correlation: Statistically significant correlation coefficient R and encouraging MAE with clinicians' grading

1. Introduction

Chronic backache in general and lower back pain in particular is considered one of the most commonly naturally occurring ailment by both the radiologists and spinal surgeon community. Lumbar spine or lower-back is the area which once affected gives rise to lower-back pain clinically termed as lumbago.

Clinical Background

In most cases, lumbago is associated with:

  • Muscular or ligament strain - caused by improper weightlifting and sitting in improper posture for prolonged duration
  • Nerve compression - radiates pain in either or both legs, commonly referred as sciatica
  • Vertebral fractures - causing intervertebral discs (IVD) to slip or herniate
  • Disc herniation - protruding discs exert pressure on nerves causing tingling pain and potential lower body paralysis

Current Clinical Practice

Diagnosing lower back pain is challenging and laborious. At present, spinal surgeons and radiologists depend on completely manual or semi-automated methods (computer-assisted software) for diagnosis, which are:

  • Laborious and time-consuming
  • Subjective in nature
  • Prone to inter-observer variation
  • Lacking reliability when performed by different clinicians

Research Motivation

The desired outcome is to offer an autonomous spinal assessment toolkit to clinicians enabling them in mass screening the images and adding vote of confidence to their traditional assessment methodology.

2. Research Objectives

  1. Composite Dataset Creation
    Introduction of an annotated lumbar spine MRI dataset in sagittal plane with relevant spinal measurements, giving the research community a composite dataset for both qualitative and quantitative assessment. To the best of our knowledge, composite dataset encompassing annotated images and spinal measurements is not currently shared for research collaboration.
  2. Robust Segmentation Solution
    Offering an accurate, consistent and robust solution for performing semantic segmentation task. A customized conventional algorithm (VBSeg) is crafted and compared with deep learning models.
  3. Automated Spinal Measurements
    Acquisition of automated spinal measurements, covering both distance-based and angular measurements including:
    • Lumbar Lordotic Angle (LLA)
    • Lumbosacral Angle (LSA)
    • Vertebral Body (VB) dimensions
    • Lumbar height measurements
    • Intervertebral body (IVB) distances
  4. Disease Classification
    Identification of presence of spinal disorders including spondylolisthesis and lumbar lordosis assessment through novel classification methodologies.

3. Clinical Evaluation Perspective

3.1 Radiologists' Viewpoint

  • The role of imaging is to localize and quantify the disease process and its effects on normal anatomy
  • MRI images offer superior image quality compared to X-ray images
  • Analysis focuses on presence of degenerative intervertebral disc diseases and related VB deformations
  • Lumbar spine (L3-L4, L4-L5, L5-S1 levels) is the most susceptible region to degenerative changes
  • Normal lumbar spine has anterior curvature ranging from 20-80 degrees
  • Excessive lordotic curve: "Sway Back" or hyper-lordosis
  • Reduced lordotic curve: "Flat Back" or hypo-lordosis

3.2 Spinal Surgeons' Viewpoint

  • Clinical examination correlates findings with imaging and radiologist reports
  • Physical examination based on severity of abnormal spine pathology
  • Detailed evaluation of spinal alignment and balance through manual analysis
  • Measurements include: LLA, LSA, pelvic incidence angle (PIA), lumbar spine height (LSH), IVD sizes
  • Surgical interventions grouped into:
    • Fusion - more invasive, VBs fused together to limit movement
    • Decompression - less invasive, removal of compressed disc/bone parts
  • Conservative treatments with physical exercises may be recommended for less severe cases

3.3 Spondylolisthesis Assessment

Definition: Spondylolisthesis is the misalignment of a vertebral body posteriorly or anteriorly due to bone stress fracture (spondylolysis).

Current Clinical Method: Meyerding Classification

  • Grading from Grade I to Grade V at equal intervals of 25%
  • Limitations:
    • Qualitative assessment prone to variation based on clinician experience
    • Dependent on VB end-plate structure
    • Compromised by osteophyte formation causing assessment variation

3.4 Lumbar Lordosis (LL) Assessment

Significance: Despite being the most significant spine parameter, assessment of spinal curvature lacks quantitative evaluation and readings are prone to variation.

Assessment Methods:

  • Modified Cobb Method - Standard for evaluating lordotic curve
    • Angle between perpendicular lines from L1 and S1 superior end-plates
    • Limitation: Relies on end-plate structure, prone to variation with osteophyte formation
  • Ishihara Index - Ratio of sum of perpendicular distances to spinal length
  • Voutsinas and MacEwen (VM) Method - Maximum orthogonal distance from corner-points
  • TRALL Method - Tangential Radiologic Assessment of Lumbar Lordosis
  • Harrison Posterior Tangent Method (HPTM) - Angle between tangent lines of VBs
  • Centroid Method (Chen) - Most calculation intensive, hence more reliable
  • Area Under the Curve (AUC) Method (Yang) - Software-assisted measurement
Clinical Standards for Lumbar Lordosis:
  • Normal Lordosis: 39¬∞ - 53¬∞
  • Hyper-lordosis: > 53¬∞ (Sway Back)
  • Hypo-lordosis: < 39¬∞ (Flat Back)

4. Dataset Development

4.1 Source Dataset

The MRI database used is publicly available at Mendeley Website, providing ample variability with total of 515 scans of subjects with symptomatic back pains.

4.2 Annotation Process

Tools and Software:

  • MATLAB R2020a Image Labeler Application - Pixel-level annotation
  • RadiAnt DICOM Viewer by Medixant - 2D slice visualization

Annotation Statistics:

  • Total Scans: 514 (1 dropped due to unsuitability)
  • Time per Image: 12-15 minutes (annotation) + 5-7 minutes (validation)
  • Labels: Six vertebral bodies - L1, L2, L3, L4, L5, S1

Annotation Workflow:

  1. Image Extraction: Mid-sagittal slices identified by expert radiologist
  2. Pixel-wise Labeling: Using polygon/smart-polygon tool with brush tool fine-tuning
  3. Validation: Expert spinal surgeons verify correctness and rectify errors

4.3 Ground Truth Creation

Bone presents the same contrast in both T1 and T2 images, therefore either T1 or T2 images are suitable for spinal measurements. Pixel spacing information is stored for conversion from pixels to millimeters.

5. Methodology

5.1 Vertebral Body Segmentation

A. Traditional Method: VBSeg Algorithm

Custom Algorithm Components:

  1. Pre-processing Stage:
    • Contrast adjustment
    • Histogram equalization
    • Detail sharpening using unsharp masking
    • Adaptive binarization
    • Image hole filling and area opening
  2. Main Framework:
    • Morphological image opening operations
    • Anisotropic filtering for edge preservation
    • Shape-based filtering using major/minor axis ratio, extent, and area
  3. Validation Stage:
    • Number of VBs checked (6 ¬± 1)
    • Iterative threshold adjustment if needed

B. Deep Learning Methods

Models Evaluated:

  • UNet - Contraction and expansion paths with skip connections
  • ResNet - Residual networks with skip connections solving vanishing gradient
  • SegNet - Encoder-decoder with max-pooling indices, computationally efficient
  • PSPNet - Pyramid scene parsing network capturing global context
  • MobileNets - Depth-wise separable convolution, smaller model size

Encoder-Decoder Combinations Tested:

  • ResNet-UNet (Best Performance)
  • ResNet-PSPNet
  • ResNet-SegNet
  • VGG-UNet
  • VGG-PSPNet
  • VGG-SegNet
  • MobileNets-UNet
  • MobileNets-SegNet

Transfer Learning: Fine-tuning method
Optimizer: Adadelta

Batch Size: 2
Epochs: 50
Train/Test Split: 70:30 (360 training, 154 testing)

5.2 Automated Spinal Measurements

Measurement Pipeline:

  1. VB Identification and Labeling
    • Region segmentation to isolate individual VBs
    • Centroid extraction for each VB (L1-L5, S1)
  2. Corner Points Computation
    • Initial assignment using bounding box corners
    • Correction using image skeletonization and nearest-neighbor calculation
    • Four corner points: superior-left, superior-right, inferior-right, inferior-left
  3. Distance Measurements
    • Lumbar Height (Method 1): L1 superior-anterior to S1 superior-anterior corner point
    • Lumbar Height (Method 2): L1 centroid to S1 centroid
    • IVB Distances: Distance between consecutive VBs at anterior and posterior sides
    • VB Dimensions: Distances between superior and inferior corner points within same VB
    • All measured in millimeters using pixel spacing information
  4. Angular Measurements
    • Lumbar Lordotic Angle (LLA): Angle between L1 superior-endplate and S1 superior-endplate
    • Lumbosacral Angle (LSA): Angle between L5 inferior-endplate and S1 superior-endplate
  5. Spinal Curve Estimation
    • Cubic spline interpolation on centroids
    • Differentiates between symmetrical and asymmetrical curves
    • Avoids overfitting using spline instead of polynomial piecewise interpolation

5.3 Disease Classification Methodologies

A. Spondylolisthesis Classification

Novel Angular Deviation Metric:

Advantages over Meyerding Classification:

  • Uses centroids instead of corner-points (more robust to end-plate deformation)
  • Quantitative assessment (not qualitative/visual)
  • Handles both anterior AND posterior displacement
  • Independent of osteophyte formation

Method:

  1. For each VB (L1 to S1), measure angle between:
    • Perpendicular line from current VB centroid
    • Line segment from current to next VB centroid
  2. If angular deviation exceeds standard deviation of mean angles ‚Üí Classify as spondylolisthesis
  3. Direction determined by:
    • Deviation less than SD of mean ‚Üí Posterior displacement
    • Deviation greater than SD of mean ‚Üí Anterior displacement

B. Lumbar Lordosis Assessment

Proposed AUC Method (Augmented Chen + Yang):

Advantages:

  • Uses centroids (Chen method) - more calculation intensive, more reliable
  • Area-based computation (Yang method) - quantitative assessment
  • Independent of corner-points and end-plate structure
  • Robust to end-plate deformities

Method:

  1. Plot spinal curve through centroids (L1 to S1)
  2. Connect superior VB centroid directly to inferior VB centroid
  3. Compute enclosed area (A·µ£) - sum of all non-zero pixels within region
  4. Classification based on empirical correlation:
    • Hypo-lordosis (Flat Back): Area smaller than normal range
    • Normal Lordosis: Area within established range œÜ

6.5 Key Evidence for Deep Learning Superiority

VBSeg vs Deep Learning Comparison:

Despite VBSeg being a thoroughly customized algorithm specifically designed for vertebral body segmentation, ResNet-UNet significantly outperforms it:

  • DSC Improvement: 0.79 (VBSeg) ‚Üí 0.97 (ResNet-UNet) = 22.8% increase
  • IoU Improvement: 0.63 (VBSeg) ‚Üí 0.86 (ResNet-UNet) = 36.5% increase
  • MPP Improvement: 66.57% (VBSeg) ‚Üí 98.31% (ResNet-UNet) = 47.6% increase

Conclusion: Provides strong evidence advocating the superiority of deep learning architectures over conventional/traditional machine learning methods for medical image segmentation.
